1. Provably Fair Algorithms (Technical Integrity)

Theme: Addressing "TrainWrecks" (The Analyst) and the "Rigged" concerns found in Reddit research.

Concept: Use a Cryptographic Hash (like SHA-256) to prove the outcome was determined before the user clicked spin.

Implementation: The server generates a "Server Seed," the user provides a "Client Seed." The combination of these two generates the result. This ensures the developer cannot change the outcome mid-spin.

Why it matters: It satisfies the "mathematically sound" takeaway and prevents "macro generation" counters.

2. Loss Aversion & The "Losses Disguised as Wins" (LDWs)

Theme: Psychology of Engagement.

Concept: When a player bets $5 and "wins" $2. Technically a $3 loss, but the machine plays winning music and flashing lights.

Research Note: Studies show the brain reacts to LDWs the same way it reacts to a real win, which is crucial for User 1 (Matt Patt) and User 5 (Joe) to keep them from getting "tired" or discouraged.

Takeaway for Iteration: The "Money counter should only show money made, not net" is a classic industry tactic to maximize time-on-device.

3. Fitts's Law in Casino UI

Theme: Physical Effort & Ergonomics.

Concept: Fitts's Law states that the time to acquire a target is a function of the distance to and size of the target.

Research Note: The "Big Red Button" and "Spacebar to Spin" requirements reduce interaction cost. For User 4 (John), large target areas are an accessibility necessity; for others, it enables "low-effort fidgeting."

Takeaway for Iteration: Map the "Spin" action to the largest visual element on the screen and the most accessible key on the keyboard.
